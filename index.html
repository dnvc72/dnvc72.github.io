Dinuguan

Deniana Clemente

The History of Dinuguan

Dinuguan is a stew from The Philippines made of diced pork, pork blood and intestines (deer sometimes substitutes) and flavored with spices, onions, garlic, siling haba chili, and vinegar. It's extremely flavorful, and almost always paired with steamed rice.
And behind such a rich dish, is a rich history.
Because pigs are native to the Philippines, the dish may be pre-Spanish colonialism. However, in former Spanish colonies like Guam, there is Fritada (Not to be confused with Fritatta), a very similar dish also flavored with pig blood. This suggests that Dinuguan may have diffused through Spanish culture to its different colonies, and was given different names and modifications. In Filipino, "Dinuguan" loosely translates to "bloodied" but Fritada comes from the Spanish verb "Fritar" which means "fried", even though Fritada isn't fried, but commonly braised or slow-cooked, like Dinuguan. The 2 colonies difference in location and language contributed to their differences. Dinuguan is also eaten in the Marianas Islands, which is very close to The Philippines and Guam. It's believed to have been introduced by Filipino immigrants, but because of its closer proximity to Guam, it's locally known as "Fritada".
One thing is for certain: Dinuguan has become such a staple as it can last up to a week without refrigeration, and in the Philippines, there has always been an economic need to use every part of a butchered animal. The dish varies by region, with different methods of cooking, in the Ilocos, Bicol, and Pampanga region. Today, it is still a beloved dish in the Philippines, and is popularly served at fiestas and buffets across the country.

Cooking Dinuguan

Though Dinuguan is a savory stew, it's memorable for its sour notes, buttery texture, and earthy taste. This is all thanks to its spices and ingredients, and the methods with which they are prepared.

Read on for a beginner-friendly recipe on how to make Dinuguan!

Ingredients

  • 1 lb pork cut into cubes
  • 1 cup vinegar
  • 2 pieces of long green pepper (siling haba chili)
  • 1 1/2 tbsp brown sugar
  • 1 onion chopped finely
  • 1 tbsp of minced garlic
  • 3 tbsp cooking oil (Any kind works!)
  • 1 cup water
  • 10 oz pork blood (Found at most butchers or Asian markets)
  • 1 pork boullion cube
  • Salt and pepper

Instructions

  1. Heat oil in a pan. Sauté garlic and onion until soft.
  2. Add pork and sauté for 5 minutes or until brown.
  3. Pour water and wait for it to boil.
  4. Add pork blood. Stir for 8-11 minutes.
  5. Pour vinegar. Add sugar and long green pepper. Cook for 12-15 minutes
  6. Season with salt and pepper as desired. Serve hot with puto (steamed rice cake)
